# 3-Year TCO Comparison (200 Users)

The self-hosted cost baseline uses the 100 MAU infrastructure figure of $448 per month from Q3, scaled to 200 users. Fixed infrastructure costs of $365 per month do not double when users double. Variable costs add approximately $10 per month in tokens and storage, giving $475 per month in infrastructure. Adding one staff member at 20% of their time at $75,000 loaded salary gives $1,250 per month and a total of $1,725 per month.

| Option | Monthly Cost (200 users) | 3-Year Total |
|---|---|---|
| Self-hosted LobeChat (infra + staff) | $1,725 | $62,100 |
| ChatGPT Enterprise | $12,000 | $432,000 |

Self-hosting is 86% cheaper than ChatGPT Enterprise and 71% cheaper than Copilot over three years including staff time. On infrastructure alone the saving against ChatGPT Enterprise is 96%. The $60 per user figure reflects the upper end of enterprise contracts. Even at $25 per user the 3-year SaaS cost of $180,000 is nearly triple the self-hosted total.

# Lock-In Analysis

Data lock-in is the most significant risk in the SaaS options. ChatGPT Enterprise and Copilot store chat history, uploads, and metrics on vendor infrastructure. Exporting at contract end requires vendor cooperation. With the self-hosted stack, all data lives in Postgres and MinIO on the EC2 instance. A full export is a single pg_dump and S3 sync command. Exit cost is negligible.

Identity lock-in with SaaS means integrating Microsoft Entra ID or OpenAI's SSO, creating dependencies that are expensive to unwind. The self-hosted stack uses Casdoor with standard OAuth 2.0 and OIDC. Switching identity providers requires changing four environment variables.

Model lock-in cuts both ways. ChatGPT Enterprise gets new models automatically, but when OpenAI deprecated GPT-4, Enterprise customers had no choice but to accept the transition. With self-hosting, model changes happen on the institution's timeline.

Tooling lock-in is the least appreciated dimension. The 11 MCP servers, Qdrant knowledge base, and Hayhooks RAG pipelines represent institutional investment lost entirely on a switch to SaaS, while also creating a capability moat SaaS products cannot replicate.

# Data Sovereignty and Compliance

ESADE operates under GDPR and Spain's LOPDGDD. GDPR Articles 5 and 6 require personal data to be processed lawfully and for specified purposes. GDPR Article 35 requires a Data Protection Impact Assessment before large-scale processing of student personal data. LOPDGDD Articles 87 to 91 establish specific digital rights in educational contexts including protections around automated decision-making affecting students.

Sending student CV content and career assessment data to OpenAI or Microsoft servers raises questions about third-party processor transfers and whether appropriate Data Processing Agreements are in place. Microsoft's EU Data Boundary programme partially addresses data residency for Copilot, but inference processing remains subject to contractual rather than architectural guarantees.

With the self-hosted stack, all data remains on EC2 in eu-west-1, with the limited exception of query text sent to DeepSeek. Full sovereignty is achievable by replacing DeepSeek with a locally hosted model. Compliance with student erasure requests under GDPR Article 17 is a single database deletion rather than a vendor support ticket with no guaranteed timeline.

# Capability Gap

**What self-hosted does that SaaS does not:**

The MCPHub layer with 11 integrated tools including Notion workspace queries, Qdrant semantic search, filesystem access, and Playwright browser automation has no equivalent in any SaaS AI product today. The custom Qdrant knowledge base loaded with ESADE-specific CV examples and recruiter feedback cannot be replicated in a SaaS product without surrendering proprietary data to the vendor.

**What SaaS does that self-hosted does not:**

ChatGPT Enterprise and Copilot offer 99.9% uptime SLAs backed by hyperscale infrastructure a single EC2 instance cannot match. They provide enterprise support, native mobile applications, and automatic model improvements requiring no operational effort.

Copilot deserves specific mention as a qualitatively different product. It is embedded into Teams, Outlook, Word, Excel, and SharePoint, offering meeting transcription, email drafting with full mailbox context, and document analysis inside native Office applications. For a university running on Microsoft 365, this workflow integration is the actual comparison a CTO should evaluate, not just standalone chat.

The TL;DR claim of equivalent functionality requires qualification. DeepSeek V4 Flash performs well for constrained RAG tasks like CV review grounded in retrieved context. For open-ended research summarisation, multilingual academic writing, or complex reasoning, GPT-4o maintains a meaningful quality advantage.

# Recommendation

Self-host if the institution has at least one staff member with proven experience in Linux server administration, Docker Compose, and AWS EC2, or budget to contract this as a managed service; has more than 30 monthly active users where the per-user cost of $14 falls clearly below the cheapest SaaS alternative; and has identified at least two workflows requiring custom tool integration or proprietary knowledge base access that SaaS cannot serve without surrendering institutional data.

Buy if the institution has no qualified technical staff, relies heavily on Microsoft 365 workflow integration, requires a vendor-backed 99.9% uptime SLA, or has fewer than 30 active users.

For ESADE specifically, self-hosting is the correct choice. Technical staff are present, the user base exceeds the economic threshold, LOPDGDD obligations strongly favour EU-resident data processing, and the MCP tool capabilities are core to the career services value proposition.

---

# Reversal Trigger

Three conditions would flip the recommendation.

First, if a SaaS provider offers a deployment model where model inference runs within the institution's own cloud account with no data egress to the vendor, similar to AWS Bedrock private endpoints, and supports custom knowledge base integration, the sovereignty argument for self-hosting no longer applies and the decision reduces to cost and operational burden alone.

Second, if the staff member responsible for the platform departs, the institution has 90 days to designate a qualified replacement, contract managed hosting to a qualified MSP, or initiate a SaaS migration. Managed hosting preserves the self-hosted capability investment while eliminating operational burden. Failure to execute any of these three paths within 90 days makes SaaS the default.

Third, if monthly active users fall below 30 for three consecutive months, the economics reverse. Fixed infrastructure costs of $365 per month represent more than $12 per user at that level, approaching the per-user price of the cheapest SaaS alternative.
